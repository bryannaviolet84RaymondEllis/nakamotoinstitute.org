Speculative Attack
Pierre Rochard

June 10, 2014

Introduction

Bitcoin naysayers1 wring their hands over how Bitcoin can possibly become mainstream. They gleefully worry that Bitcoin will not make it across the innovation chasm for a variety of reasons:

  1. It's too complicated
  2. The security is too hard to get right
  3. Existing and upcoming fiat payment systems are or will be superior
  4. It's too volatile
  5. The government will ban it

The response from the Bitcoin community is to either endlessly argue over the above points2 or to find their inner Bitcoin Jonah3 with platitudes like:

  • It's not Bitcoin the currency that I care about, it's Bitcoin the technology.
  • It would be better if the block chain technology were used by banks and governments.
  • Bitcoin should continue to be a niche system for the bit-curious, it's just an experiment.
  • Fiat and Bitcoin will live side-by-side, happily everafter.
  • Bitcoin is the Myspace

The above sophisms are each worth their own article, if just to analyze the psycho-social archetypes of the relevant parrots.

Thier's Law

Historically, it has been good, strong currencies that have driven out bad, weak currencies. Over the span of several millennia, strong currencies have dominated and driven out weak in international competition. The Persian daric, the Greek tetradrachma, the Macedonian stater, and the Roman denarius did not become dominant currencies of the ancient world because they were "bad" or "weak." The florins, ducats and sequins of the Italian city-states did not become the "dollars of the Middle Ages" because they were bad coins; they were among the best coins ever made. The pound sterling in the 19th century and the dollar in the 20th century did not become the dominant currencies of their time because they were weak. Consistency, stability and high quality have been the attributes of great currencies that have won the competition for use as international money.

Robert Mundell, Uses and Abuses of Gresham's Law in the History of Money

Bitcoins are not just good money, they are the best money4. The Bitcoin network has the best monetary policy5 and the best brand6. We should therefore expect that bitcoins will drive out bad, weak currencies7. By what socio-economic mechanism will bitcoins become the dominant currency? Which fiat currencies will the first to disappear? These are the interesting questions of the day, as the necessary premises for these questions are already established truths4.

Fiat Bleed, Garden-variety Bitcoinization

Bitcoins' current trend is to increase in value on an exponential trend line as new users arrive in waves. Two factors drive this:

  1. Reduction in information asymmetry - people are learning about Bitcoin and coming to the realization that bitcoins are indeed the best money. Possible overlapping motives:
    • ADHD - compulsive novelty fetichism encouraged by our post-war consumer culture.
    • FOMO - fear of missing out, see Regret Theory and ingroups, aka greed for money and social status.
    • PISD - post-internet stress disorder, aka "disruption", "next big thing", "internet of money"
  2. Increasing liquidity - it is much less expensive and much more convenient to buy bitcoins today than a year ago. One can reasonably predict that this will also be the case a year from now. Why? Because it is profitable. Why? Because people want bitcoins, see above.
